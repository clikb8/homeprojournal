7 Things Every Homeowner Should Know About Home Equity Sharing

HP
HomeProJournal Staff | Home Finance Guide

Local Homeowners: Home equity sharing is one of the fastest-growing ways to tap your home's value — but it's also widely misunderstood. Before you refinance, open a HELOC, or do anything else, here's what you actually need to know.

Key Takeaways
  • Requires 620+ FICO, 35%+ equity, primary residence (no manufactured homes)
  • It's not a loan — no monthly payments and no interest
  • You keep your home, your life, and your existing mortgage rate
  • You share a portion of your home's future change in value at the end
  • How you can access your home equity, and how to take action today

1 It's Not a Loan

This is the big one. Home equity sharing isn't borrowing. You receive cash today, and in return, you agree to share a portion of your home's future change in value. There's no interest accruing in the background.

Because it's not a loan, it doesn't show up on your credit report as debt, and it doesn't affect your debt-to-income ratio. That's a big deal for homeowners who are already carrying a mortgage.

2 There Are No Monthly Payments

Because it isn't a loan, there's no monthly bill and no interest. Instead, you settle the agreement — repaying the original investment plus or minus the agreed share of your home's change in value — when you sell or reach the end of the term. Nothing is owed monthly in the meantime.

For homeowners on a fixed income, carrying high-interest debt, or simply trying to protect their monthly cash flow, this changes the entire calculation. You have no new payment eating into your budget every month.

3 You Keep Your Home and Your Mortgage

You remain on title and keep living in your home. Your existing mortgage and the rate you locked in stay in place — exactly as they are. That's a big reason homeowners choose this over a cash-out refinance, which would force them to start a new loan at today's higher rates.

4 You Share in Future Value — Up or Down

When you settle, the amount is based on your home's value at that time. If your home appreciates, you share some of that gain. In some programs, if your home value has decreased, the company may share a portion of that loss as well — but this varies by agreement and may be capped or conditional. Always review your specific contract terms.

This aligns the company's incentives with yours — they want your home to go up in value just as much as you do.

5 How Much You Can Access Varies

The amount depends on your home's value, your equity needs, and your location. Some homeowners can access a meaningfully large sum up to program limits. The only way to know your number is through the specific provider and process.

Most programs offer between 5%–15% of your home's current market value, with investment amounts ranging from $30,000–$500,000 depending on the company.

6 Eligibility Is About Your Home, Not Just Your Credit

Because it's based on your home's equity rather than a traditional loan, qualification can be more flexible than a refinance — which can help self-employed and fixed-income homeowners. Specific requirements are set by the provider.

Generally, you'll need a credit score of 620 or higher, at least 35% equity remaining in your home, and it must be your primary residence — manufactured homes are not eligible. Many providers do not require W-2 income verification, making this especially accessible for retirees and self-employed homeowners.

7 Checking Costs Nothing

Seeing what you could access is free, takes about a minute, and doesn't commit you to anything. From there, a specialist can walk you through the details so you can decide if it's right for you.

Bottom line: Home equity sharing offers no monthly payments and no interest — in exchange for a share of your home's future change in value. Terms and eligibility are set by the provider and vary.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is home equity sharing a loan?

No. It's not a loan and it doesn't appear as debt on your credit report. You're selling a small stake in your home's future appreciation in exchange for a lump sum today. There's no APR, no monthly payment, and no interest charges.

Will I have a monthly payment or interest?

No. That's the core difference from a HELOC or cash-out refinance. With equity sharing, there is no monthly payment and no interest rate. You settle only when you sell, refinance on your own terms, or reach the end of the term.

Do I still own my home?

Yes, completely. You remain the full legal owner of your property. The company has a lien that gets settled at a future exit event, but you live in your home normally — your name is still on the deed.

How much equity can I access?

Investment amounts range from $30,000–$500,000, typically 5%–15% of your home's current market value. You'll need a 620+ FICO score, 35%+ equity, and a primary residence (no manufactured homes). Checking your estimate takes about a minute and is free.

What can I use the cash for?

Anything you choose — debt payoff, home renovations, medical bills, education, retirement income, starting a business. There are no restrictions on how you use the funds once they're wired to your account.

Are there any fees?

Yes. There is a one-time transaction fee — typically around 3.9% of the investment amount — deducted at closing. There are no monthly fees, no ongoing interest charges, and no prepayment penalties. When you settle (at sale or end of term), you repay the original investment amount plus or minus the agreed share of your home's change in value. Total cost depends on how your home's value changes over time.

Does checking my estimate cost anything or hurt my credit?

The estimate check is free. Many providers use a soft credit pull for the initial check — confirm the specific process with your provider, as practices vary. A hard inquiry typically only occurs if you choose to proceed with a full application.
